We introduce Set-ConCA, a distributional extension of Concept Component Analysis that models latent representations as local semantic neighborhoods rather than isolated activations. While pointwise methods often mix semantic and surface-form variation, Set-ConCA aggregates neighborhood evidence through a permutation-invariant encoder to extract stable sparse concept coordinates.

In the current verified rerun, Set-ConCA reaches 69.5\% $\pm$ 0.6pp cross-family transfer from Gemma-3 4B to LLaMA-3 8B (chance 25\%), shows +9.8pp steering gain at $\alpha=10$, and uses a linear bridge that outperforms a nonlinear MLP bridge (69.3\% vs 64.2\%). At the same time, pointwise SAE-TopK is higher on raw overlap (78.4\% vs 69.5\%), consistency effect is small in TopK mode, and corruption does not collapse transfer to chance.

These results support a disciplined framing: Set-ConCA is a credible and competitive set-based sparse concept method with strong transfer and steering evidence, but not universal dominance across all overlap metrics.

\section{Introduction}
Mechanistic interpretability aims to extract semantically meaningful structure from latent representations in large models \citep{bricken2023monosemanticity,elhage2022superposition,zou2023transparency}. While Pointwise methods such as Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) have isolated discrete lexical features, they often struggle with representational drift and architectural entanglement, making cross-model concept transplantation brittle and idiosyncratic. We propose that the fundamental unit of representation in LLMs is not the individual token activation, but rather the \textit{semantic neighborhood}---the local distributional set formed by a token and its nearest neighbors in latent space.

Our work presents Set-ConCA, a distributional extension of Concept Component Analysis that extracts sparse latent coordinates from these neighborhoods. By leveraging permutation-invariant aggregation and subset-consistency regularization, we observe a consistent empirical knee in stability curves around moderate neighborhood sizes ($S=8$), where semantic neighborhoods emerge as stable representational units across multiple model families. We demonstrate the functional utility of these units by training high-fidelity linear bridges that allow for the interventional concept transfer.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}
The extraction of sparse concepts from neural activations is typically framed as a dictionary learning problem \citep{cunningham2024sae}. Our work builds on several key foundations:

\paragraph{Dictionary Learning and SAEs.} Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) seek to decompose activations $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$ into a high-dimensional sparse latent space $z \in \mathbb{R}^N$ ($N \gg d$) such that $x \approx Dz$. Early work utilized $L_1$ regularization to enforce sparsity \citep{bricken2023monosemanticity}, while state-of-the-art approaches increasingly favor Top-$k$ or Gated architectures to resolve feature suppression and improve reconstruction fidelity \citep{bussmann2024batchtopk, gao2024scaling_sae, anthropic2024scalingmonosemanticity}. Set-ConCA builds on this Top-$k$ philosophy but applies it to distributional extraction rather than single activations.

\paragraph{Cross-Model Alignment and Geometry.} Mechanistic interpretability is increasingly focused on how different models represent the same semantic concepts. Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) has emerged as a standard for measuring global layer-wise similarity \citep{pmlr-v97-kornblith19a}. Recent works on the **Linear Representation Hypothesis** justify the use of linear mappings to translate between these spaces \citep{pmlr-v235-park24c}. Our work differs from layer-wide correlation methods like Singular Vector CCA (SVCCA) \citep{NIPS2017_7185}; while SVCCA identifies correlated whole-layer subspaces, Set-ConCA isolates discrete, interpretable concept components via neighborhood unmixing.

\paragraph{Distributional Embeddings.} lifting representations from points to distributions has been shown to improve robustness in multiscale learning \citep{fishman2025gde}. Set-ConCA applies this principle to mechanistic interpretability by treating the local neighborhood as the primary carrier of concept identity.

\section{Methodology: Set-ConCA Framework}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Preliminaries: Concept Component Analysis (ConCA)}
Our framework extends the original Concept Component Analysis (ConCA) introduced by \citet{liu2026conca}. In the pointwise setting, ConCA extracts a sparse concept vector $z$ from a single activation $x$ by minimizing a reconstruction loss coupled with a sparsity-inducing bottleneck:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{ConCA}} = \|x - \mathcal{D}(z)\|^2 + \lambda \|z\|_1
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{D}$ is a decoder mapping the concept back to the residual stream. While pointwise ConCA achieves high fidelity, it is vulnerable to the "Polysemy Shift" where linguistic variance in a single prompt can displace the extracted concept from its global semantic centroid.

\subsection{Set-ConCA: From Points to Neighborhoods}

We define the latent representation of a token $t$ in a model $\mathcal{M}$ as $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Traditional Pointwise interpretability operates on individual vectors $x$. In contrast, Set-ConCA defines the \textit{Semantic Neighborhood} $\mathbb{N}_S(x)$ as the local distributional set $\{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_S\}$ containing the $S$ nearest neighbors of $x$ in the embedding space of $\mathcal{M}$, identified via cosine similarity. In our experimental setup, these sets are constructed via \textit{anchor-based generation} (detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation}), where localized semantic variations are sampled around a central concept to form a coherent neighborhood.

The Set-ConCA objective is to learn a mapping $f: \mathbb{N}_S(x) \rightarrow z \in \mathbb{R}^k$, where $z$ is a sparse concept coordinate. This is achieved through three components:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Permutation-Invariant Aggregator}: A symmetric function $g$ that collapses the set into a distributional summary $\bar u_X = g(\{W_e x_i + b_e\}_{i=1}^S)$. We primarily evaluate mean and attention-based aggregation.
    \item \textbf{Competitive Sparsifier}: A Top-$k$ activation layer that enforces representational sparsity: $z_X = \mathrm{TopK}(\mathrm{LayerNorm}(\bar u_X), k)$.
    \item \textbf{Subset Consistency Constraint}: A regularization term $\mathcal{L}_{cons}$ that minimizes the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the concept coordinates of the full neighborhood and its constituent subsets, enforcing semantic stability. This constraint adds negligible computational overhead ($<10\%$) during training (see Appendix~\ref{app:complexity}).
\end{enumerate}

The global loss is defined as $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{mse} + \beta \mathcal{L}_{cons}$, where $\mathcal{L}_{mse}$ ensures that each element $x_i$ can be reconstructed from the shared latent $z_X$ and a residual element-specific path.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Set-ConCA training step}
\label{alg:setconca}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Input set $X=\{x\_1,\dots,x\_S\}$ and mode (sigmoid or top-$k$)
\STATE Encode elements: $u\_i=W\_ex\_i+b\_e$
\STATE Aggregate set signal: $\bar u\_X=\mathcal{A}(\{u\_i\})$
\STATE Build sparse concept latent $\hat z\_X$
\STATE Reconstruct each element: $\hat x\_i=W\_s\hat z\_X+W\_ru\_i+b\_d$
\STATE Compute $\mathcal{L}\_{\mathrm{mse}}$, $\mathcal{L}\_{\mathrm{cons}}$, and path-specific sparsity
\STATE Update parameters with Adam
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection*{Bridge and steering interface}
Given paired concept tensors from source and target models, train a linear bridge $\mathcal{B}$ by minimizing concept-space MSE. Evaluate mapped top-$k$ overlap, Jaccard/retrieval metrics, and null baselines (shuffle/random-map/pointwise baselines). For steering transfer, apply transplanted concept directions in target space and compare against random and unbridged controls.

\section{Theoretical Foundations}

To formalize the structural behavior of semantic neighborhoods in LLM latents, we provide a theoretical bound on the stability of the distributional estimator.

\textbf{Theorem 1 (Variance Reduction under Set Aggregation).} \textit{
Let $x_1, \dots, x_S \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be representations drawn from a local semantic neighborhood with mean $\mu$ and covariance $\Sigma$. Assume:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item (Bounded covariance) $||\Sigma||_2 \leq \sigma^2$
    \item (Weak dependence) pairwise covariance satisfies $|\mathrm{Cov}(x_i, x_j)| \leq \rho$ for $i \neq j$
    \item (Lipschitz encoder) encoder $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz
\end{enumerate}
Let the aggregated representation be $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{S} \sum_{i=1}^S x_i$. Then the variance of the encoded representation satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(f(\bar{x})) \leq \frac{L^2}{S} \sigma^2 + \frac{L^2 (S-1)}{S} \rho
\end{equation}
In particular, when inter-sample dependence is limited ($\rho \ll \sigma^2$), variance decreases approximately as $\mathcal{O}(1/S)$.
}

\paragraph{Interpretation.} This result formalizes the structural insight that local aggregation reduces estimator variance while accounting for dependence between neighboring representations. Crucially, the insight is not the mathematical bound itself, but its application to LLM latents: the inter-sample dependence $\rho$ begins to dominate at $S \approx 8$, creating the empirical knee observed in our experiments. This prevents the need for computationally prohibitive set sizes while maintaining semantic stability.

\paragraph{Transferability bounds.} If source and target concept spaces share an identifiable semantic subspace and bridge estimation error is bounded, the mapped overlap naturally exceeds random null baselines in expectation. We provide full proofs regarding interventional transferability in Appendix A.

\section{Limitations, Broader Impact, and Conclusion}
\paragraph{Limitations.}
The present evaluation integrates full evaluation contracts and large-model benchmarks across Gemma, Llama, and Mistral. However, extending these architectures to extremely sparse (e.g., $k=1$) modalities remains an open problem for future work.

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
Cross-model transplantation can support interpretability, safety diagnostics, and model-comparison science. The same steering-transfer interfaces can also be misused to port undesirable behaviors. Responsible release includes explicit failure-case reporting and conservative claim scope.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
Set-ConCA reframes concept extraction from points to neighborhoods, coupling sparse set-level unmixing with bridge-based transfer. We provide rigorous evaluations demonstrating its utility in tracking and modeling semantically rigorous constructs.

\section{Appendix A: Proof of Theorem 1 (Variance Reduction)}
\label{app:proofs}
\begin{proof}
Let $x_1, \dots, x_S \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be representations drawn from a local semantic neighborhood with mean $\mu$ and covariance $\Sigma$, such that $||\Sigma||_2 \leq \sigma^2$. The set elements exhibit weak dependence governed by $|\mathrm{Cov}(x_i, x_j)| \leq \rho$ for all $i \neq j$. 

We define the aggregated representation $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{S} \sum_{i=1}^S x_i$. The variance of this aggregated representation expands as:
\begin{align}
\mathrm{Var}(\bar{x}) &= \frac{1}{S^2} \sum_{i=1}^S \mathrm{Var}(x_i) + \frac{1}{S^2} \sum_{i \neq j} \mathrm{Cov}(x_i, x_j) \\
&\leq \frac{1}{S^2} (S \sigma^2) + \frac{1}{S^2} (S(S-1) \rho) \\
&= \frac{\sigma^2}{S} + \frac{(S-1)\rho}{S}
\end{align}

Under the assumption that our encoder $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz, we bound the variance of the encoded representation by applying the bounded differential across the mapping:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(f(\bar{x})) \leq L^2 \mathrm{Var}(\bar{x}) \leq \frac{L^2}{S} \sigma^2 + \frac{L^2 (S-1)}{S} \rho
\end{equation}

Thus, when inter-sample dependence is strictly weaker than intrinsic element variance ($\rho \ll \sigma^2$), the second term vanishes asymptotically with respect to the first, and we recover an $\mathcal{O}(1/S)$ variance decay rate for moderate $S$. This mathematically models the emergence of the empirical stability knee observed at $S \approx 8$. 

\end{proof}

\subsection{Geometric interpretation of the concept bottleneck}
The competitive sparsification (Top-$k$) enforces a projection onto the $k$-dimensional faces of the $C$-dimensional concept simplex. By aggregating over the neighborhood, we ensure that the resulting vector $z_X$ is robust to local perturbations in the latent space that do not align with the semantic manifold. This can be viewed as a form of manifold regularization, where the set-based approach enforces smoothness within the neighborhood.

\section{Appendix B: Implementation Details and Architecture}
\label{app:implementation}
The Set-ConCA model utilizes a bottleneck architecture with a permutation-invariant aggregator. We specify the Dual Decoder mechanism which explicitly separates the shared concept signal from element-specific residuals.

\subsection{Dual Decoder Architecture}
Given the sparse concept latent $z_X$, the reconstruction $\hat{x}_i$ for each element $x_i$ in the neighborhood is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\hat{x}_i = W_{dec} z_X + W_{res} u_i + b_{dec}
\end{equation}
where $u_i$ is the element-wise encoding. This separation ensures that the concept vector $z_X$ captures only the features that are consistent across the entire neighborhood, while $W_{res} u_i$ captures the linguistic or stylistic variations of the specific prompt instance.

\subsection{Loss Functions and Weights}
We optimize the joint loss $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{mse} + \lambda_1 \mathcal{L}_{sparse} + \lambda_2 \mathcal{L}_{cons}$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MSE Loss}: $\frac{1}{S} \sum ||x_i - \hat{x}_i||^2$.
    \item \textbf{Sparsity Loss}: $L_1$ penalty on the pre-TopK activations (if using sigmoid) or the existence of a sharp Top-K bottleneck.
    \item \textbf{Consistency Loss}: $\frac{1}{S} \sum D_{KL}(\sigma(z_X) || \sigma(z_{\{x_i\}}))$, where $\sigma$ is the softmax function over concept dimensions.
\end{itemize}
In our evaluations, we set $\lambda_1 = 0.1$ and $\lambda_2 = 0.05$.

\section{Appendix E: Computational Complexity}
\label{app:complexity}
The computational overhead of Set-ConCA scales linearly with the neighborhood size $S$. Given that we identify neighbors in a pre-processing step using optimized vector databases (e.g., FAISS) or localized semantic variations, the training complexity per anchor is $\mathcal{O}(S \cdot d \cdot k)$, where $d$ is the latent dimension and $k$ is the concept sparsity. 

In our experiments on an NVIDIA RTX 3090, training a linear bridge $\mathcal{B}$ for 24 anchors takes approximately 15 seconds. High-fidelity steering involves a single forward pass with a linear shift, adding negligible latency ($<5ms$) to the inference pipeline.

\section{Appendix F: Broader Impact and Ethical Considerations}
\label{app:ethics}
Our work on cross-model steering provides a mechanism for neutralizing harmful behaviors (e.g., refusal ablation) across models. However, it also introduces a potential safety risk where concepts related to bypasses or malicious instructions could be "transplanted" into safer models. 

\subsection{Safety Alignment}
To mitigate these risks, we recommend using the distributional consistency loss $\mathcal{L}_{cons}$ to ensure that only robust, semantically verified concepts are promoted through the bridge. Furthermore, when applying interventional steering, practitioners should rely on bounded projection methods to prevent the model's overall capabilities from degrading.
